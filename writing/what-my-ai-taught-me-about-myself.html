What My AI Taught Me About Myself

March 2026 · Josh Stoner

I pointed my personal AI memory system at my own data. What came back was more honest than I expected.

I've been building something called stonerOS—a personal AI memory system that sits on top of Claude. It tracks my career history, preferences, projects, and working patterns across sessions. But until this week, it only knew what I told it.

So I tried something different: what if it could learn from my actual behavior instead?

Your Mac already has access to most of your iPhone data through iCloud—browsing history, photos, app usage, reminders, voice memos. All SQLite databases. I had Claude build a set of Python scripts to pull patterns from each one and feed them into stonerOS's routing pipeline. Everything runs locally—no data leaves the machine.

Here's what came back.

I'm a Night Builder

My app usage data shows peak activity between 1am and 3am. Not doomscrolling—VS Code. 91 hours of active use in 90 days, nearly double the next app (Perplexity at 52 hours). Development represents 48% of my total tracked screen time.

I've always known I'm a night owl. But there's a difference between knowing that and seeing "peak sessions: 1–3am" confirmed across three independent data sources. There's also a clear afternoon dip where I switch from building to communicating, then a second wind after 10pm.

What I learned

This isn't a habit to fix—it's when my brain actually works. Schedule deep work for late night. Keep meetings in the afternoon. Lean into the rhythm instead of fighting it.

The Hobby That's Actually a Skill

My browsing data categorized 68% of my web activity as "Other." When I actually looked at what that meant: ~780 visits to collectibles marketplaces—eBay, Whatnot, PopMart, specialty jewelry sites. Almost as much as social media.

I collect designer toys and vinyl figures. I knew I spent time on this, but the data showed how much of it is research—not just browsing. I have an eBay scraper that tracks market prices into a SQLite database. I'm watching wholesale and secondary markets. It's systematic.

What I learned

The skills I use for collecting—market analysis, data pipelines, pattern recognition—are the same ones I use in my professional work. Hobbies aren't separate from your professional identity. They're often the same muscles applied differently.

My Focus Has a Shape

I'm a builder in deep-focus mode. My geographic footprint is essentially two places: my apartment in Brooklyn and my family's place in rural Pennsylvania. My communication is concentrated in a tight inner circle—a handful of people I talk to daily, deeply, and consistently. My photos are 78% from home: plants, my space, my dog Theodore.

None of this was news to me. But seeing all of it together, quantified, I noticed something I hadn't named before: narrow inputs, deep engagement, high consistency. It's the same pattern everywhere—few apps used heavily, few people messaged frequently, few places visited repeatedly.

What I learned

This is how I operate. It works well for building—but it also means things like networking and exploring new spaces don't happen on their own. They have to be intentional.

My Old Systems Are Time Capsules

My Apple Notes—86 of them—haven't been touched since November 2025. They contain resume drafts, job search prompts, design philosophy statements, and shower thoughts from a pre-AI version of me.

One note from 2023: "Designers are observers and curators to the creation of a finalized product, not the dictators." Another: "My design starts with Why." The ideas are still mine, but they were written in a different role, at a different company, with different tools. It's interesting to see where your head was before you had a system to build on those thoughts.

What I learned

The thinking was always there. What changed is having a system that compounds it instead of letting it sit in a notes app.

The Meta-Lesson

Honestly, none of this was a surprise. I already knew all of it. I knew I was a night builder. I knew my focus was narrow. I knew my hobbies used the same muscles as my work.

But knowing something and seeing it in data are different. "I think I'm a night person" becomes "my peak cognitive window is 11pm–3am, confirmed across three data sources." One is a feeling. The other is something I can actually plan around.

All of this was already on my phone. I just never looked at it this way before.
